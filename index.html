🕌 Islamic Aviation Heritage
The Golden Age of Science that Made Flight Possible
📜 Pioneers Timeline
810 — 887 CE
Abbas ibn Firnas
عباس بن فرناس
First controlled flight attempt in Córdoba, Spain. Designed wings from silk and eagle feathers, glided from a mountain — 600 years before Leonardo da Vinci's designs.
✈️ Connection: Every plane you see owes a debt to his courage
780 — 850 CE
Al-Khwarizmi
الخوارزمي
Father of Algebra ("al-jabr"). Invented algorithms — the word "algorithm" comes from his name. Every flight computer, GPS, and autopilot runs on his mathematics.
🧮 Right now: calculating ETA for 0 planes overhead
965 — 1040 CE
Ibn al-Haytham
ابن الهيثم
Father of Optics. His "Book of Optics" (Kitab al-Manazir) pioneered the scientific method and understanding of light. Radar, head-up displays, and navigation screens are built on his science.
👁️ Connection: Every radar screen and cockpit display
858 — 929 CE
Al-Battani
البتاني
Master of trigonometry and spherical geometry. His precise astronomical calculations made celestial navigation possible. Great circle routes — the shortest paths planes fly — rely on his work.
📐 Right now: great circle routes guiding planes
973 — 1048 CE
Al-Biruni
البيروني
Calculated Earth's circumference with stunning accuracy using trigonometry and a mountain. Air navigation depends on knowing Earth's exact shape and size.
🌍 His measurement: ~6,339km radius (actual: 6,371km — 99.5% accurate!)
1136 — 1206 CE
Al-Jazari
الجزري
Father of mechanical engineering. His automated machines, camshafts, crankshafts, and programmable devices laid the foundation for the engines that power every aircraft.
⚙️ Connection: Every jet engine traces back to his mechanisms
800 — 880 CE
Fatima al-Fihri
فاطمة الفهري
Founded the University of Al-Qarawiyyin in Fez, Morocco in 859 CE — the world's oldest existing university. She created the system of higher education that trained astronomers, mathematicians, and engineers for centuries.
🏛️ Connection: Every pilot and engineer trained at universities she pioneered
936 — 1013 CE
Al-Zahrawi
الزهراوي
Father of modern surgery. His 30-volume medical encyclopaedia described over 200 surgical instruments. Aviation medicine and every aircraft's emergency medical equipment follows his systematic approach to saving lives.
⚕️ Connection: In-flight medical kits carry his legacy
1100 — 1165 CE
Al-Idrisi
الإدريسي
Created the Tabula Rogeriana — the most accurate world map for 300 years. He mapped continents, trade routes, rivers, and mountain ranges. Every flight chart and navigation map descends from his cartographic tradition.
🗺️ Connection: Flight routes follow paths he first charted
1903 CE
Wright Brothers
First powered flight at Kitty Hawk, built on centuries of aeronautic science — standing on the shoulders of giants.

🧭 Astrolabe → GPS: Same Science, 1000 Years Apart
🌙
Astrolabe (800 CE)
Measured star angles to find latitude & longitude. Islamic scholars perfected it for finding qibla direction and prayer times. Used star positions, horizon reference, and trigonometry.
📡
GPS (2000 CE)
Measures satellite signals to find position. Uses the same mathematical principles — triangulation, spherical geometry, and algorithms — all pioneered by Islamic scholars.
The Math Connection
position = f(angles, spherical_trig, algorithms)
Both astrolabe and GPS solve the same problem: "Where am I on a sphere?" The mathematics of Al-Battani (trigonometry), Al-Khwarizmi (algorithms), and Al-Biruni (Earth measurements) bridge both technologies.

🔬 Physics of Flight — Islamic Foundations
Bernoulli's Principle (roots in Ibn al-Haytham's fluid dynamics)
P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant
Air moves faster over the curved top of a wing, creating lower pressure above. This pressure difference is lift — the force that holds every plane up. Ibn al-Haytham's studies of how light and fluids behave through different media helped lay the groundwork.
Great Circle Distance (Al-Battani's Spherical Trigonometry)
d = R × arccos(sin φ₁ sin φ₂ + cos φ₁ cos φ₂ cos Δλ)
The shortest path between two points on Earth is a curve, not a straight line. Al-Battani's spherical trigonometry made calculating these routes possible — every flight plan uses this formula.
